"a style of software testing that emphasizes the personal freedom and responsibility of the individual tester to continually optimize the quality of his/her work by treating test-related learning, test design, test execution, and test result interpretation as mutually supportive activities that run in parallel throughout the project."

Source: Wikipedia

Exploratory testing  is a form of testing that I had heard of but not really been motivated enough to go an explore (excuse the pun). However this changed once I had been to a Test Retreat meet-up down in Bournemouth where some of the testers were discussing its merits. (If you go to http://www.softwaretestingclub.com you will be able to see a list of events that take place where testers meet up and talk testing. I would highly recommend this as you can learn an awful lot and be sociable at the same time). So curiosity got the better of me and I decided to go and buy a book called "Explore It!" by Elisabeth Hendrickson.  Having bought the book It took about another couple of months to start reading it, which I did in the relaxing atmosphere by a pool in Rhodes :)

In this post I will talk through some of the concepts mentioned in the book that I found interesting as well as giving my thoughts on the book in general.

Interesting Bits

Charters
A charter is basically  a statement of what exactly you want to explore whilst testing. In the book the format Elisabeth suggests is:

Explore (target) with (resources) to discover (information)

So an example would be something like:

"Explore billing with variations in sequences and data to discover ways a customer could be doubled billed"
(This is an example from the book)

From a testing perspective this is a great idea as it gives you focus enough not to get distracted on other areas of the application but freedom to go and play with the software to see what you can find. Working from traditional test cases I find can be a boring and tedious experience but this gives you the chance to explore and use a testers inquisitive nature, but it also keeps you within the boundaries of the functionality that is being tested.

Personas
A persona is a type of user that uses the system you are testing. Using personas in testing prompts you to interact with the software with a set of assumptions about how the user will interact with it. For example an experienced user may use shortcut keys whereas a new user may use the mouse to click on everything. The concept of personas will add another dimension to your testing from how you approach what you are testing to what you are actually testing, as different users could use the system differently. From my own personal experience I test the software how I would use it so I am therefore restricting the opportunities to find bugs that may be discovered If I had tested in a different Persona.

Nightmare Game
Now this is linked to charters as the outcome of this game can be the basis for charters. In a nutshell you should gather together a group of people and tell them to imagine being in there normal morning routine. They then imagine checking the news and seeing a headline of a major new story that your piece of software has caused. So for example:

* Shopping Cart adds £300 to order
* 1,000 people have there credit card details stolen
* Hundreds of deliveries delivered to the wrong addresses

Once the group have come up with some headlines they chose a particular headline to work on and start to brainstorm the contributing causes. Then they get into groups of twos and threes and and get them to chose any of the contributing factors and then identify charters that might reveal the problem.

I think this is an excellent exercise and one I plan to run at some stage in the future.

The Book
Being blunt and to the point; Buy this book, it is excellent.

It gave me a great introduction to exploratory testing and has encouraged me to incorporate some of the concepts into my day to day work. It is well written and I would recommend this book to any tester whether they use exploratory testing or not. You may not be able to totally change the testing process in your organisation but by using a few of the techniques in this book you may well discover some bugs that have laid dormant for some time.
