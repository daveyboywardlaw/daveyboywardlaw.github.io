I made a deal. We were moving house and I said to my step-daughter that If she was not a pain in the neck during the whole process I would buy here either a toy or a pet. I was hoping she would pick the toy but she picked a pet. That pet ended up being a gecko. Now if you don't know what one of those is, here's a picture:




Now like all living things, geckos need a few essential things to survive:

  1. A heat source
  1. Food & Water

Now Geckos need to shed their skin, fast forward a few months and the Gecko starts shedding its skin - happy days!! It had a lovely heat mat to generate heat and lots of meal worms to eat and water to drink. However, a few weeks later when the weather turned a little cooler, she was not shedding her skin very well. So after doing a little research and finding out that they can lose toes if there skin does not shed properly I went and bought a bigger, better heat mat. This helped, and the Geckos skin was starting to shed a little easier but it was sktill not coming off as easily as it should be. So, having done some research about helping geckos to shed there skin, I would, on a daily basis bath the gecko in warm water and using a cotton wool bud try and gently remove the skin from the effected areas. Although tricky this helped the Gecko shed and I think she actually quite enjoyed it. The problem with this is that it is not really a job you want to have to do everyday and there is always risk that she may escape. It wasn't like I hadn't spent enough money, so off I went to buy a heat lamp which I was hoping would be the final piece in the jigsaw to helping her shed her skin without the need for human intervention. So once I managed to attach the heat lamp to the vivarium the skin started to shed a lot easier and we had a happier gecko and the baths stopped.

Now how does a gecko not shedding its skin relate to testing? Well in this story the gecko wasn't getting enough of what it needed to thrive. It was getting what it needed (heat, food/water) but not necessarily enough or in the right way.

I love a definition so here is the definition of thrive From https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/thrive)

(of a child, animal, or plant) grow or develop well or vigorously

Prosper; flouris
Well that was a nice short one… :)

If you work a standard 9-5 day you are basically spending more time (awake) with you colleagues than you do your spouse, so enjoying and getting what you want out of that time is important. Everyone wants to prosper (not necessarily in a monetary way) otherwise life would be a pretty joyless existence and surely you want to prosper at something that you spend most of your conscious time doing?

So as a tester are you getting what you need to thrive in your role? Only you can answer that question as every individuals needs are different. It could include things like:

  • Learning new testing skills
  • Learning to write code
  • Learn about the domain you are working in
  • Interacting with clients throughout the whole project
  • Developing skills to enable me to move onto a more senior role in the future
  • Developing your testing coaching skills

The list goes on and on......

If you are not thriving then maybe you need to ask yourself why and what you need to thrive?  What will get you up in the morning and make your work enjoyable?  Maybe you don't want to thrive and that's fine but just don't complain when people start to notice. If you are not thriving this could potentially reflect in your work and get noticed by your peers and colleagues. It may be that you are like the gecko - on paper you are getting what you need but you are still not happy. It could be that you are getting nothing of what you need to help you thrive - unlike the gecko you will live whereas the Gecko wouldn't last long with no food, water or heat.  So once you have identified what you need to thrive you need to go after them.....

It may be that you have to start with small steps but once you know what you need you have something to work towards. Once you know what you need, think about places where you can go and get the resources that will help you. I have a (sometimes unhealthy habit) about learning to write code and I have utilized the developers at work and started to write a mobile app using Xamarin forms. The developers are experienced in C# so I can use them to help me when I get stuck or have a question. Even little 'making a cup of tea in the kitchen' chats can help develop knowledge and enable you to move onto developing your skills that little bit more. Yes it may be very small steps to start with but each little step helps towards a larger goal.

There are so many resources out there for testers to go out and learn something new. You have great resources like the Ministry of testing as these can be used to help you grow and learn (Once you know what you need)

So if you feel like your work is not giving you what you need, write a list of things you need to thrive. Then see if you role can give you the things, If it can, great!! Then stop reading this blog and start planning how you are going to learn/incorporate these things into you work routine. If your work cannot give you those things that you need then maybe you need to think about the role you are currently in….
